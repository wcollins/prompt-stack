# The Cloud Gambit - The Effort Illusion: Why AI Tools Reward Expertise, Not Shortcuts
## Guest: Hank Yeomans | Senior Director, Product - Conceal
## Recording: March 3, 2026

---

### Cold Open

There's this split happening in tech right now - on one side you've got people claiming AI writes production software while you grab coffee, and on the other, folks dismissing everything AI touches as slop. The truth is somewhere in the middle, and it's way more interesting than either camp wants to admit. Today we're digging into that messy middle ground with someone who's been building with these tools, calling out the hype, and has real opinions about what actually works.

---

### Guest Welcome

Hank, welcome to The Cloud Gambit. You've got one of those careers I love - started in networking and infrastructure, went deep on security at Cisco, and now you're leading product at a browser security startup. Plus you've been stirring up some really good conversations online about AI and engineering. Glad to have you on.

### Discussion Flow

**Context:** Hank's career arc goes from infrastructure and networking through Cisco security to Senior Director of Product at Conceal. Classic technical-to-leadership path but with some interesting turns.
**Lead-in:** So Hank, I always like to start with the path - because nobody's career goes in a straight line. You came up through networking and infrastructure, spent time in Cisco's security world, and now you're leading product at a startup.
**Questions:**
- Walk us through that journey - what pulled you from infrastructure into security, and then from engineering into product?
- When you made that jump to product leadership, what skills from your networking days carried over, and what did you have to completely relearn?

#### Topic 2: An Engineering Mind in a PM Leader's Body
**Context:** Hank still builds - he's not a product leader who left the keyboard behind. His blog project building AI agents with Claude's API directly shows he's still hands-on.
**Lead-in:** One thing that jumped out to me about you is that you're not the kind of product leader who stopped building. You're still writing code, still getting your hands dirty.
**Questions:**
- How does being technical - like actually technical, not "I read Hacker News" technical - change the way you make product decisions?
- Do you ever have to fight the urge to just build it yourself instead of speccing it out for the team?

#### Topic 3: What Conceal Is Actually Doing
**Context:** Conceal does Browser-Native SSE (Security Service Edge). They raised a $26M Series B. The pitch is that security belongs in the browser, not in a proxy. Unsponsored episode - keep it about the technology and market trend, not the product.
**Lead-in:** Let's talk about the space you're in. Browser security is having a moment right now, and the approach Conceal takes is fundamentally different from the traditional proxy model.
**Questions:**
- For folks who aren't deep in the browser security world, what's the core idea behind putting security directly in the browser instead of routing everything through a proxy?
- Where do you see the browser security market heading? That Series B suggests the market agrees there's something here.

#### Topic 4: The Spotify Post and the "Best Devs Don't Code" Narrative
**Context:** Spotify claimed their top engineers haven't written code since December thanks to AI. Hank had thoughts about this. Source: TechCrunch article from Feb 12, 2026.
**Lead-in:** Alright, let's get into it. Spotify made headlines recently claiming their best developers haven't written a line of code since December - all thanks to AI. That got a lot of reactions, and you were one of them.
**Questions:**
- When you saw that Spotify headline, what was your gut reaction? What's real there and what's corporate messaging?
- There's a difference between "our best engineers don't write code" and "our best engineers use AI to write better code faster" - do you think that nuance got lost?

#### Topic 5: Kelsey Hightower's "Slop Machine" Post
**Context:** Kelsey Hightower posted about AI being a "slop machine." Hank's response was sharp - "Why shouldn't something require a lot of work to get something good out of it?" His position is that domain knowledge is the actual differentiator.
**Lead-in:** So Kelsey Hightower - someone a lot of our listeners respect - put out a post calling AI a slop machine. And your response really stuck with me. You said something like, "Why shouldn't something require a lot of work to get something good out of it?"
**Questions:**
- Unpack that for us. When you say the effort is the point, what does that actually look like when you're using AI tools day to day?
- Do you think the "slop" problem is an AI problem or a people problem?

#### Topic 6: Matt Shumer's "Something Big Is Happening" and the Counterpoint
**Context:** Matt Shumer claimed he walks away from AI and returns to superior work. Fortune published a counterpoint saying his assumptions are flawed. Good tension to explore.
**Lead-in:** On the other end of the spectrum, you've got Matt Shumer writing "Something Big Is Happening" - basically saying he walks away and comes back to better work than he could do himself. Then Fortune came back and said his assumptions are fundamentally flawed.
**Questions:**
- Where do you land on this? Is Shumer describing the future or selling a fantasy?
- What's the most dangerous assumption people are making about AI productivity right now?

#### Topic 7: "Cauliflower Rice Is Rice (It's Not)"
**Context:** Hank's analogy about "vibe coding a billion-dollar company in a weekend" vs. the reality that projects like OpenClaw took years of experience. The difference between a demo and a product.
**Lead-in:** You had this great line - I'm paraphrasing - about cauliflower rice not actually being rice. And you tied it to this idea that you can't just vibe code a billion-dollar company in a weekend.
**Questions:**
- Tell us about that analogy. What's the gap between what people demo on Twitter and what actually ships to production?
- OpenClaw is a good example you brought up - what's the story there that people miss when they just see the "built in a weekend" headline?

#### Topic 8: Two Kinds of People in Technology
**Context:** Hank frames it as the "this is slop" camp vs. the "I made it exist, now I'll make it better" camp. Interesting lens on how engineering orgs are splitting.
**Lead-in:** You've talked about there being two kinds of people when it comes to AI in tech - the "this is all slop" crowd and the "I made it exist, now I'll iterate and improve it" crowd.

**Context:** Hank predicts AI will expose people who never had genuine curiosity or depth. This is a spicy take with real implications for hiring and team building.

**Questions:**
- What does incurious mediocrity look like in practice? Give us an example from an engineering org or a product team.

#### Topic 10: Building AI Agents the Hard(er) Way
**Context:** Hank has a blog project where he's building an AI agent using Claude's API directly instead of a framework. Hands-on technical exploration. Source: hankaas.wordpress.com

**Context:** Hank says his networking and Linux skills are "never going away." Relevant for our audience of infrastructure engineers wondering what to invest in.
